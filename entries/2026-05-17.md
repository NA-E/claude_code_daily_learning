**Creative Friction — Why AI Speed Is a Liability When Direction Is Undefined**
Adobe and Parsons School of Design conducted a full-semester study of how AI affects the creative process. Key finding from Brooke Hopper, Senior Principal Designer at Adobe: "The system is operating faster than your ability to engage with it critically." Students with *clear direction* incoming got AI acceleration; students with *vague direction* got AI-defined direction. The mechanism: AI is designed to complete patterns. If your instruction is incomplete, the model fills in the gaps — it keeps generating anyway. Students consistently grabbed the steering wheel at the *end* of projects, not the beginning, to preserve authorship. Source: TechTiff Substack (techtiff@substack.com, May 16).
**Why it matters for you:** This applies directly to Claude Code sessions. Without a clearly defined success condition before you start, Claude's output defines your direction instead of your intention. The `/goal` feature and TASK.md structure (May 16 entry) are the technical answer: they force direction *before* the model starts accelerating.
**Action:** Before every Claude Code session, write one sentence: "When this session is done, [specific output] will exist and be verifiable by [check]." If you can't write that sentence in under 60 seconds, you don't have enough direction. Don't open Claude Code until you can — the model will otherwise fill your gaps with its own.
