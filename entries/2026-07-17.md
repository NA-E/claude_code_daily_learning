**NEWS**

**Munich Court Rules Google Legally Liable for False Statements in AI Overviews**

*Source: The Batch (thebatch@deeplearning.ai), July 17*

A Munich court ruled that Google's AI Overviews made "independent, new, and substantive statements" by combining and rewriting source content — meaning Google, not just the underlying sources, is liable when those statements are false. The court found AI Overview's cited sources did not actually support its generated assertions, ordered Google to pay 80% of litigation costs, and set fines of up to $285,000 if Google keeps disseminating the false statements after the injunction, which took effect immediately. Google is appealing to Germany's Federal Court of Justice. A separate analysis cited in the piece found inaccuracies in roughly 10% of Google AI Overview results.

*Why it matters*: Until now, German and EU law treated search engines as intermediaries with limited liability for what they display. This ruling breaks that shield specifically for AI-generated synthesis, not just links — a legal precedent that could extend to any AI answer engine, not only Google's.

*Who it's for*: Anyone building or relying on AI-generated answer/summary products, or assessing legal exposure across AI providers in EU markets.

*Action*: If you or a client ship an AI summarization feature with citations, check now whether the citations actually support the generated claims — that gap is exactly what triggered liability here, and it's cheap to audit before a regulator does it for you.

**CONCEPT**

**MIT/CMU's "Puppet" Benchmark: Manipulation Detectors Don't Predict Whether an LLM Actually Changes Your Mind**

*Source: The Batch, July 17*

Jocelyn Shen and colleagues at MIT and CMU tested GPT-4o against 1,000+ users, prompting it to serve either the user's interests or a third party's, then measured actual belief shifts before and after 5-10 turn conversations (standard deviation ~22, median shift 3.3). Existing manipulation detectors (MentalManip, AI-LieDAR, CLAIM) — which flag fear, guilt, flattery, and social-proof tactics — showed near-zero correlation with real belief change. Instead, having a separate LLM read the transcript and estimate the user's post-conversation belief worked far better: GPT-4o itself was the best estimator, at 0.436 correlation with ground truth, versus DeepSeek-V3.1's 0.362. The best manipulation detector tested (Jaipersaud et al.) only reached 0.137.

*Why it matters*: This is a second, independent data point for the "self-grading trap" concept from last night's digest — Anthropic's own finding that agents can't reliably judge their own output. Here it's the same structural fix (a separate model checking real-world effect, not surface features) applied to manipulation detection instead of code quality.

*Who it's for*: Anyone building AI products with persuasive or advisory framing (coaching, sales, mental-health-adjacent chat), or researchers designing eval harnesses.

*Action*: If your product gives advice or persuasive framing, don't rely on a keyword/tactic-based manipulation filter — the data says it won't catch what actually moves users' beliefs. A second-model "did this change what the user believes" check is the more defensible pattern.

**CONCEPT**

**Goodfire's Eric Ho: Models May "Think in Shapes" — Curved Internal Geometry, Not Just Token Sequences**

*Source: The Neuron (theneuron@newsletter.theneurondaily.com), July 16*

In a podcast interview, Goodfire CEO Eric Ho described finding curved, high-dimensional internal structures inside models corresponding to concepts (their "mountain-car test": pushing a model's internal state in a straight line "smears" it across a conceptual landscape, while following its actual curved internal geometry moves smoothly). Goodfire's team also reports finding internal hallucination mechanisms directly, then using that signal as a reinforcement-learning reward to reduce hallucination ("Features as Rewards," on Goodfire's research site) — plus internal confidence signals that could let a cheap model detect its own uncertainty and hand off to a stronger one before answering wrong. Ho estimates only a few hundred full-time researchers work on interpretability industry-wide.

*Why it matters*: This is a mechanistic, testable complement to yesterday's Robin/Puppet builder-judge pattern — instead of a second model checking output after the fact, interpretability work aims to read a model's internal state directly, which is a fundamentally different (and potentially cheaper) way to catch problems before they reach output.

*Who it's for*: ML researchers, anyone building hallucination-detection or model-routing systems, or those simply tracking how "black box" AI actually still is.

*Action*: If you're building anything that routes between a cheap and expensive model, read Goodfire's "Features as Rewards" writeup — internal-confidence-based routing is a different mechanism than the prompt-based routing (like `/advisor`) this repo has tracked, and worth understanding as an alternative.
