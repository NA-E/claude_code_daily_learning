**AI Delegation Engine — 6-Prompt System for Reclaiming 10+ Hours Per Week**
*Source: The AI Break (theaibreak@substack.com)*

The AI Break published a complete tutorial for building a delegation system inside ChatGPT. The 6-prompt sequence: (1) **Time Excavator** — walk the AI through your week day-by-day; it returns a TASK INVENTORY table with columns: Task | Category | Est. hours/week | How often | Energy drain (Low/Med/High); (2) **Only-You Filter** — every task gets an OWNERSHIP VERDICT: ONLY-YOU, COULD-BE-YOU, or SHOULD-NOT-BE-YOU; (3) **Four-Bucket Sorter** — every non-essential task routed to Delegate, Automate, Kill, or Keep; (4) **Leverage Calculator** — ranks tasks by hours×cost×pain score; (5) **Handoff Brief Generator** — builds a runnable handoff doc per delegated task; (6) **Weekly Ritual** — protects reclaimed hours from creeping back. No new tools required — this is prompts-only.

*Why it matters for you*: You've been building Claude Code routines since May. This framework gives you a principled inventory of what those routines should be absorbing from your week — rather than guessing.

*Action*: Open Claude or ChatGPT right now. Run Prompt #1 (Time Excavator) using your last typical week. You'll have a ranked delegation plan in ~45 minutes.

*Action*: For any consulting, freelance, or advisory work you do: draft what an outcome bundle would look like before your next pricing conversation. Cover at least 2 dimensions — e.g., speed of delivery + accuracy of result, or cost reduction + quality retention.

**CONCEPT**

**"Hard to Eval Is a Product Smell" — Hamel Husain**
*Source: Parlance Labs (hamel_husain@parlance-labs.com)*

Hamel Husain (AI product consultant, ex-GitHub/Fast.ai) published a post at hamel.dev/blog/posts/eval-smell/ with a sharp argument: when teams say "our product is hard to eval," that's a product design flaw, not a testing limitation. His reasoning: artifacts that are hard for your team to verify are usually hard for users to verify too — and that creates distrust even when the AI output is correct. He walked through before/after interfaces for 3 products he advised: an internal AI data agent answering business questions, a PE lesson-plan generator for K-12 teachers, and a workers' comp tool that drafts 50-page medical reports. Each before/after shows design patterns for verifiability: checkable artifacts, diffs showing what changed, surfaced assumptions, and inline citations.

*Why it matters for you*: If you're building or planning any AI tool, the verifiability of outputs needs to be a UI design criterion, not an afterthought. A user who can't quickly check Claude's output won't trust it — regardless of accuracy.

*Action*: Read hamel.dev/blog/posts/eval-smell/ and identify one design pattern from the before/after examples. Apply it to something you're building: add diffs, surfaced assumptions, or inline citations to at least one AI output surface.

*Source: The Rundown AI (news@daily.therundown.ai)*

Meta published Brain2Qwerty v2 — a non-invasive brain-scanning system that decodes **full sentences** (vs. v1 which decoded single characters). *Why it matters for you*: Non-invasive BCI went from single-character to full sentences in one generation. Open-sourcing v1 and v2 means the research community can now build on this foundation — the pace of improvement will accelerate.

*Action*: File as a watch item. If you're interested in assistive tech or novel human-computer interfaces, bookmark the Meta AI blog and the open-sourced repo.

**Wardship — Real-Time Safety Layer for AI Companion Apps (Product Concept)**
*Source: IdeaBrowser (notifications@mail.ideabrowser.com)*

IdeaBrowser's Idea of the Day: "Wardship" is a proposed B2B safety layer for AI companion apps. Unlike keyword filters that miss context, Wardship screens conversations in real time by reading the emotional arc of a relationship over weeks — catching self-harm signals, age-inappropriate content, and compliance red flags as they escalate rather than only at the moment they peak. When a threshold triggers: routes user to crisis resources, blocks the exchange, logs everything for legal/compliance audit. Audit trails are pre-formatted for regulators. *Why it matters for you*: "Safety as a product layer" is becoming a real B2B market — sold to AI companies rather than end users. The picks-and-shovels angle is strong as AI companion apps face increasing regulatory pressure in the EU and US.

```
You are an elite operations consultant and time-and-motion analyst who has helped
hundreds of founders escape the day-to-day of their business.

My context:
- My role: [YOUR TITLE, e.g. solo founder / PM / consultant]
- My business: [WHAT YOU DO + STAGE]
- Team I can lean on: [WHO YOU HAVE, or "just me"]

Your job is to interview me and rebuild a realistic picture of my week.

Do this:
1. Ask me to walk through a typical week, day by day, in plain language.
   Prompt me for the boring and invisible stuff too: email, Slack, approvals,
   "quick questions," meetings, rework, and context-switching.
2. Turn my answers into a TASK INVENTORY table:
   Task | Category | Est. hours/week | How often | Energy drain (Low/Med/High)
3. Add rows for anything I clearly forgot based on a business like mine.
4. Total my hours and tell me honestly: is this sustainable or burnout-bound?

Ask interview questions first. Do not build the table until I answer.
```

Answer the interview honestly for 20 minutes. Then run Prompt #3 (Four-Bucket Sorter): every non-essential task gets routed to Delegate, Automate, Kill, or Keep. Total time: ~45 minutes. Output: a ranked list of exactly which tasks your Claude Code automation and other AI systems should absorb from your week — by hours saved, not by vague priority.

This is high-leverage because it converts the general sense that "AI should be doing more" into a specific, prioritized action list with the tasks you've been doing manually.
